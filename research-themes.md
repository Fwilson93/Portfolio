## Research Themes

My research investigates how planetary interiors evolve from molten states to chemically and structurally stratified cores, and how these processes control magnetic field generation and long‑term habitability.

A central theme of my work is understanding **freezing, nucleation, and differentiation** in extreme planetary environments.

---

## Inner‑core nucleation and slurry layers

The onset of inner‑core growth remains a fundamental open problem in geophysics. I use molecular dynamics simulations and non‑equilibrium thermodynamics to quantify nucleation barriers in liquid iron alloys and to develop physically consistent models of inner‑core formation.

This includes work on:
- Homogeneous and heterogeneous nucleation in Fe‑light‑element liquids  
- Slurry layers and compositional stratification at the top of the inner core  
- Links between core thermodynamics, seismic structure, and geomagnetic evolution  

---

## Molecular dynamics of planetary liquids

I apply classical and first‑principles molecular dynamics to investigate the structure, dynamics, and freezing behaviour of silicate, carbonate, and metallic liquids at extreme pressure and temperature conditions.

These simulations provide constraints on:
- Transport properties (diffusion, viscosity)  
- Free energies and entropies of crystallisation  
- Chemical partitioning during differentiation  

---

## Magma oceans and early planetary evolution

Following giant impacts, terrestrial planets are expected to undergo deep magma‑ocean phases. My work explores how these magma oceans cool, crystallise, and chemically evolve, and how these processes influence core formation and volatile retention.

---

## Magnetic field generation and habitability

By coupling core thermodynamics to dynamo theory, I investigate how chemical energy sources — including light‑element precipitation — can sustain planetary magnetic fields over geological timescales.

This research provides constraints on:
- The longevity of Earth’s geodynamo  
- Magnetic histories of Mercury and terrestrial exoplanets  
- Conditions required for surface habitability
